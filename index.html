Rwanda: Land of a Thousand Hills

Rwanda at a Glance:
Known as the "Land of a Thousand Hills," Rwanda is a vibrant nation in East Africa. It has emerged as a global leader in environmental conservation and safety.

With its capital, Kigali, being one of the cleanest cities in Africa, Rwanda offers a unique blend of modern urban life and breathtaking natural scenery.

Why visit? Beyond the landscapes, Rwanda’s true beauty lies in its people—resilient, welcoming, and united toward a sustainable future.

Nyungwe Forest National Park

Nyungwe is the second national park in Rwanda with which African Parks has partnered. In 2020, building on a successful partnership with RDB in Akagera National Park, African Parks was invited by the government to sign a 20-year agreement to partner in managing Nyungwe National Park. Together, African Parks is working to restore and conserve the park’s biodiversity, support local communities and establish sustainable tourism to secure its long-term future. Today, Nyungwe is becoming both an ecologically healthy and financially self-sustaining national park, while fostering a strong sense of conservation ownership among neighbouring communities.

park management

Nyungwe National Park is managed by African Parks in partnership with the Rwanda Development Board (RDB). A non-profit conservation organisation, African Parks takes on responsibility for the rehabilitation and long-term management of protected areas in partnership with governments and local communities. It currently manages 23 national parks and protected areas in 13 countries. RDB is the government agency responsible for managing Rwanda’s national parks and protected areas.

Akagera National Park

Founded in 1934 to protect animals and vegetation, Akagera national park is the largest protected wetland in Africa. It was named after the Akagera River which flows along its eastern boundary. The park used to cover over 2,500 sq. km but in 1997; it was reduced in size by close to 50%. A lot of the land was reallocated to refugees returning to Rwanda after the civil strife of the genocide. Before 1997, many refugees returning to Rwanda had settled in the area and the conservation area was harmed by poaching and cultivation. In 2009, Rwanda Development Board and Africa Parks signed a joint management agreement in which the Akagera management company (AMC) was established to help both bodies manage the park. AMC is therefore responsible for the day to day management of the park. Wildlife in Akagera National Park When we talk about the wildlife in this national park, the Akagera is the best savanna destination in Rwanda where you can enjoy game viewing during the thrilling game drives and boat rides during which you will get a chance to see a variety of large mammals and a profusion of birds as highlighted below. Animals in Akagera national park The Akagera national park serves as home to a great diversity of wild animals among which are: elephants, lions, buffalos, hyenas, zebras, giraffe and several antelopes like the bushbucks, roan antelope, Topis, klipspringer, Oribis, Cape eland, water-buck, duikers and impalas. There are a number of primates species found within this park such as the olive baboons, Vervet monkeys, and the nocturnal bush babies. The shallow waters of Lake Ihema are home to a large number of crocodiles and hippos which can best be viewed during boat rides.

how to get akagera national park

The distance from Kigali to Akagera national park is about 108 kilometers and the park can be accessed by either road a short flight. By road it takes about 2 hrs and 30 minutes drive from Kigali the capital city vial the town of Rwamagana to the southern gate of the park (the northern gate is used for exit only at the moment). For those who would not wish to drive can opt for a short flight into Akagera and these services are provided by the Akagera Aviation.

Volcanoes National Park

Volcanoes National Park in northwestern Rwanda protects the Rwandan slopes of the Virunga Mountains, famous for gorilla trekking, golden monkey tracking, and hiking its five volcanoes (Karisimbi, Bisoke, Muhabura, Gahinga, Sabyinyo). Established in 1925 as Africa's first national park, it's a vital sanctuary for endangered mountain gorillas and golden monkeys, offering unique biodiversity within montane rainforests, grasslands, and swamps, and serves as a major base for primate research and conservation.

Lake Kivu

Lake Kivu is a stunning, large freshwater lake in the African Great Rift Valley, forming a natural border between Rwanda and the Democratic Republic of Congo, known for its deep, emerald waters, surrounding volcanic mountains, and unique dissolved gases like methane, which is being harnessed for energy, making it a major tourist spot for activities like boating and relaxing in lakeside towns like Rubavu and Karongi, despite its potential danger from gas eruptions.

Genocide Memorial Site

Genocide memorials, like the prominent Kigali Genocide Memorial in Rwanda, are solemn places for remembrance, education, and reconciliation, honoring victims of atrocities like the 1994 Rwandan Genocide against the Tutsi, serving as final resting places for hundreds of thousands and featuring exhibitions, archives, and survivor support, promoting peace education to prevent future genocides globally.

View Hotels

Purpose of the Memorials

The memorials serve as places of remembrance, learning, and healing. They remind the world of the consequences of hatred and the importance of unity and tolerance.
